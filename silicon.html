Silicon (Si)

Group 14 · Period 3 · p‑block · metalloid

Discovery & basic data

History (RSC): Identified in 1824 by Jöns Jacob Berzelius (Sweden) by heating potassium fluorosilicate with potassium. Named from Latin silex (flint).
Statistics: Atomic number 14 · Mass 28.0855 u · Stable isotopes: 28Si (92.2%), 29Si (4.7%), 30Si (3.1%). CAS 7440-21-3.

Position & origin

Location: Group 14 (carbon group), period 3, p‑block – a metalloid.
Natural abundance: Second most abundant element in Earth’s crust (~28%). Found as silica (SiO₂) and silicates. Major sources: quartz, sand.

Physical properties

  • State at 20 °C: solid, dark grey with bluish tinge, metallic lustre.
  • Density: 2.33 g·cm⁻³.
  • Melting point: 1414 °C.
  • Boiling point: 3265 °C.
  • Hardness: brittle, 6.5 Mohs.
  • Semiconductor: electrical conductivity increases with temperature (typical for metalloids).

Chemical properties

  • Reactivity: Relatively inert at RT; attacked by hot alkalis and halogens. Combines with oxygen at high T to form SiO₂.
  • Configuration: [Ne] 3s² 3p².Full configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p²
  • Electronegativity: 1.90 (Pauling).
  • Ionisation energies (kJ/mol): 786, 1577, 3231.
  • Common oxidation states: +4 (most stable, e.g. SiO₂, silicates), also +2 (rare).

Bonding & ions

Bonding: Covalent network in elemental form (diamond structure). Forms predominantly covalent compounds; in silicates, bonding is covalent/ionic.
Ions: Does not form simple cations; exists as oxyanions (silicates, e.g. SiO₄⁴⁻). No stable monatomic ions in solution.

Applications

  • Semiconductors: Base material for microchips, transistors, solar cells (high‑purity silicon).
  • Glass & ceramics: Silica (SiO₂) for windows, containers; also in porcelain, enamels.
  • Silicones: Polymers with alternating Si–O backbones; used in sealants, lubricants, medical implants.
  • Alloys: Ferrosilicon in steelmaking to improve strength and deoxidise.
  • Construction: Cement and concrete rely on silicate aggregates.

Name

From Latin silex (flint) because of its occurrence in flint (a form of silica). The name was coined by Thomas Thomson (1817) and adopted by Berzelius.
